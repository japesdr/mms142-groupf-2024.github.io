Part 1: Disruptive Technologies

Before diving into Music Streaming Services, let’s discuss the concept of Disruptive Technologies.

What Are Music Streaming Platforms?

Music streaming services are advanced platforms that function like online libraries, storing millions of songs online and sending them instantly to internet users all around the world through various devices and connections (Miquido, 2024).

Additionally, these platforms are highly intelligent, allowing algorithm processes to be more advanced and curated to each individual user, paving the way for personalized playlists, suggested songs, and adapt to each user's preferences. All of these transformed how we listen to music, making it more accessible, convenient, and tailored to individual tastes.

HOW

DOES IT

WORK?

Upon hitting the play button, quite complex procedures are initiated within the background of these applications. Let’s get to know music streaming services functions in the perspective of both us, listeners, and the developers themselves.

From the POV of Listeners

When we click and play a song, let’s say, on Spotify, this is a form of request which is called a Stream. Specifically, Audio Streaming is a process to listen to media over a wireless Internet connection, without having to download it first (Kandari, 2024).

This request data is broken down into smaller chunks called packets, reaching the source server of Spotify. The streaming service then delivers the requested data (the song played) also in packets so that the user can immediately start listening to pre-buffered music (Patsukhov, 2022).

Additionally, through the use of caching, replaying music can be faster streamed music becomes quicker and more efficient. This reduces the need to re-fetch the same data from the server.

From the POV of Developers

Before sending out music to their users, music streaming services need to go on a process called Music Licensing. This is where they obtain licenses from copyright owners (i.e, the artist/s, songwriter/s, and record label) of the songs they want to include in their libraries.

To secure these rights, music streaming services typically pay licensing fees to allow them to legally distribute music in their platforms. According to Chelles (2023), music streaming services need to have a license agreement upon 3 of the following criteria.

1. Geographic: In what particular countries can the music be streamed?

2. Temporal: How long will the music remain available on the platform?

3. Payment: How much will the music streaming service pay the copyright owners per user stream?

HOW

DID IT

EVOLVE?

Music streaming services did not just fell out of a coconut tree, they are the result of a long history of innovation in how we record, store, and listen to sound. Starting with Edison’s phonograph and Berliner’s microphone in the 19th century, the evolution continued through vinyl records in the 40s, cassette tapes in the 60s, and optical discs of the 70s.

However, as technology advances and with the advent of the Internet in the 80s, people began to desire for faster and more convenient ways to access music on their computers. This led to a surge in illegal music downloads and piracy in the early 2000s, with users digitally sharing music files they had uploaded from physical copies they had purchased (Pastukhov, 2022).

Thus, this opened for a new innovation gap and a potential growth of a new market, which made way for the development of the music streaming services we know today. With this, let’s take a look at the timeline of music streaming services.

THE GOOD AND THE BAD

While music streaming services have revolutionized the music and radio broadcasting industries, their disruptive impact can be a double-edged sword. As these platforms continue to evolve, they bring about both positive advancements and negative challenges that shape the way we create, consume, and value music today.

Today, music streaming platforms account for 84% of revenue, and the music industry has grown more than 10% in a year to reach $17.5 billion worldwide. Increased Success & Visibility of Independent Artists

Independent artists, who are not signed in big record labels and has limited resources, gained more recognition beyond mainstream music.

For instance, Grammy-award-winning rapper Chance the Rapper and singer-songwriter Lauv both gained widespread digital success without being signed to major record labels (Havens, 2017; Herstand, 2019). As of November 2024, both artists have achieved over 10 billion streams on Spotify alone (Kworb, 2024). This proves that music streaming services have been a significant boost for the visibility and success of independent and smaller artists, offering them a fairer playing field with other established artists.

Increased Cultural Exchange

Furthermore, music streaming services have fostered significant international cultural exchange, as demonstrated by the worldwide popularity of K-pop, commonly known as the “Hallyu Wave”.

In fact, global streams of K-pop have increased by 362%, with Southeast Asian streams rising by 423% from 2018 to 2023 (The Korea Times, 2024). K-pop acts such as BTS and BLACKPINK have been able to penetrate international markets without the need for traditional barriers found in non-digital platforms. This trend highlights how music streaming services have expanded the reach of non-Western music, contributing to a more diverse range of discussions and a broader global music landscape.

Reduced Cost and Increased Creative Freedom

The blockchain technology integrated in music streaming services result in the creation, distribution, and monetization of music without any hierarchical constraints. This change allows artists to have more control over their work by eliminated numerous middlemen, significantly reducing costs (L'Hommedieu, 2024).

Moreover, small artists can now release their music independently without costly significant label deals, which gives them more creative freedom and the ability to manage their own careers (Butler, 2019). This shows how music streaming platforms have democratized the music industry, empowering all artists regardless of their background.

Unjust Artist Compensation

While music streaming platforms generate substantial revenue, the amount artists earn per stream remains minimal. For example, in 2014, Spotify paid artists an average of $0.0038 per stream, which increased to $0.035 per stream in 2020 (Aswad, 2024). This is a decent increase but still criticized as unsustainable, especially for smaller or independent artists.

Also, Spotify CEO Daniel Ek revealed that artists are not being paid directly, but rather their record labels and publishers (Alford, 2023), which may leave artists with a fraction of the earnings.

Biased Algorithms

Despite earlier claims that music streaming services help independent artists, the saturation of these platforms today has resulted in biased algorithms that put smaller, independent artists at a disadvantage.

To elaborate, Horgenmiller (2022) notes that Spotify’s algorithm exhibits a popularity bias. In simple terms, their algorithm tends to favor songs, artists, and genres that are already popular and mainstream, presenting them more frequently in users' recommendations. With the platform’s per-stream royalty model, established artists continue to dominate, while smaller artists struggle to grow and earn profit.

Contribution to Environmental Degradation

Despite the absence of physical mediums, digital music consumption and streaming services still contribute to environmental harm. Surprisingly, ∼4% of the world's carbon emissions come from the energy required for servers, data transmission, and infrastructure supporting music streaming (Carbometrix, 2022).

Additionally, Sharon George of Keele University (2021, as cited in CBC News) pointed out that streaming just five hours of music is equivalent to the carbon footprint of one plastic CD case, while 17 hours of streaming equals the carbon footprint of one vinyl record.
